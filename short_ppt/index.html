Linear ICA via Optimal Transport

Ashutosh Jha  ·  Joachim Grammig  ·  Michel Besserve  ·  Simon Buchholz

  • Linear Independent Component Analysis (ICA) recovers mutually independent sources from their observed linear mixture.
  • Optimal Transport (OT) theory gives a metric, the Wasserstein distance, for the cost of the optimal path from one probability distribution to another.
  • Our Question: Can an OT-based metric serve as the contrast function for ICA?

The problem: linear ICA

Theorem (Linear ICA Identifiability). [Jutten & Herault 1985; Comon 1994]

Let $\mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ be observed data and $\mathbf{S}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ latent sources. Assume (i) $\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{A}\mathbf{S}$ with $\mathbf{A}$ invertible, (ii) $s_1,\dots,s_d$ mutually independent, and (iii) at most one $s_i$ Gaussian. Then $\mathbf{A}$ is identifiable up to column permutation and scaling.
  • Identifiability is only up to permutation (source order) and scale/sign, inherent ambiguities of the model.
  • Why "at most one Gaussian"? Any linear combination of Gaussians is Gaussian, and the multivariate Gaussian is rotation-invariant: once decorrelated, there is no preferred direction left to find.
  • So: with $\ge\!2$ non-Gaussian sources, there is a unique direction to search for.

Centering, whitening, and the search space

  • Center: $X \leftarrow X - \mathbb{E}[X]$.
  • Whiten: linear map so $\operatorname{Cov}(\widetilde{X}) = I$.
  • Whitening removes correlation but not higher-order structure; the cloud becomes isotropic in covariance, not necessarily Gaussian-shaped.
  • What's left to search: a rotation $\mathbf{b}$ on the orthogonal group $\mathcal{O}$ [Absil et al. 2008].
  • $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{b}\in\mathcal{O}} \ \mathrm{Contrast}\big(\mathbf{b}^\top \widetilde{X}\big)$
  • Contrast is some measure of independence. What should it be?

The Cardoso idea: independence as non-Gaussianity [Cardoso 2022]

  • Project the joint distribution $P_Y$ onto the Product manifold $\mathcal{P}$ (closest independent distribution) and the Gaussian manifold $\mathcal{G}$ (closest Gaussian).
  • Two KL Pythagorean identities, shared hypotenuse, give Cardoso's unified theorem (shown left).
  • After whitening, $\operatorname{Cov}(Y)=I \Rightarrow C(Y)=0$, simplifying the identity (shown left).
  • $G(Y)$ is rotation-invariant $\Rightarrow$ minimizing mutual information $\equiv$ maximizing $\sum_i G(Y_i)$, the marginal non-Gaussianities.
  • Great! Now we need a contrast for non-Gaussianity in place of independence, where do we find it?

Optimal Transport: the basics

  • Monge's problem [Monge 1781]: move a pile of mass onto a target shape at minimal cost, originally via a deterministic map.
  • A push-forward map $T$ satisfying $T_\#\mu = \nu$ realizes that idea: it moves all the mass of $\mu$ onto $\nu$. But for many pairs ($\mu,\nu$ discrete with mismatched point masses, for instance) no such single deterministic map exists.
  • Kantorovich relaxation [Kantorovich 1942] fixes this: let mass split across a transport plan (coupling) $\pi\in\Pi(\mu,\nu)$, a joint distribution with marginals $\mu$ and $\nu$. A plan always exists (a push-forward is the special case where $\pi$ concentrates on the graph of $T$).
  • How this captures Monge's cost: $W_p$ is defined as the cost of the cheapest transport plan, the minimum, over every valid coupling, of the expected cost $\|x-y\|^p$ of moving each unit of mass: $$W_p(\mu,\nu) = \left(\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(\mu,\nu)} \int \|x-y\|^p \, d\pi(x,y)\right)^{1/p}$$
  • $W_p$ is a true metric on the space of probability distributions [Villani 2003]: it satisfies the triangle inequality, unlike many density-based divergences.

Wasserstein ($W_2^2$) as contrast

  • Why $p=2$, not $p=1$? Empirically, the squared penalty in $W_2^2$ acts as a structural smoother on the optimization landscape: its gradient decelerates continuously as the candidate direction rotates. $W_1$'s linear penalty instead produces a jagged, volatile derivative on finite samples that stalls first-order solvers.
  • In 1D, Brenier's theorem [Brenier 1991] + Caffarelli regularity guarantee the optimal transport map is monotone, reducing $W_2^2$ to a quantile-matching integral: $$W_2^2(\mu,\nu) = \int_0^1 \big|F_\mu^{-1}(t) - F_\nu^{-1}(t)\big|^2\,dt$$
  • Why monotone helps: a monotone map preserves order, so it must send the $k$-th smallest sample of $\mu$ to the $k$-th smallest value of $\nu$, rank for rank. Concretely $T = F_\nu^{-1}\circ F_\mu$: applying $F_\mu$ to a sample is just its rank divided by $N$ (the empirical CDF, found by sorting), then $F_\nu^{-1}$ looks up the matching target quantile.
  • Neither step ever evaluates a density $f_\mu(x)$ or its gradient, so there is no kernel density estimate or bandwidth to tune, just sorting [del Barrio et al. 1999; Peyré & Cuturi 2019].
  • Our contrast: $W_2^2$ distance to a standard Gaussian $\Gamma = \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The generic objective from Slide 3 becomes concrete: $$\hat{\mathbf{b}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{b}\in\mathcal{O},\ \sum_i b_i^2=1} \ W_2^2\big(\mathbb{P}_{\mathbf{b}^\top \widetilde{X}},\, \Gamma\big)$$

Why maximizing $W_2^2$ recovers sources

Theorem ($W_2$ upper bound). For mutually independent sources $Z_1,\dots,Z_d$ and weights $\mathbf{b}=(b_1,\dots,b_d)$ with $\sum_{i=1}^d b_i^2=1$ (the unit sphere):

$$W_2^2(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z}, \Gamma) \le \sum_{i=1}^d b_i^2\, W_2^2(Z_i, \Gamma)$$

Theorem (strict inequality). If at most one source is Gaussian and $\ge\!2$ weights $b_i$ are nonzero, the bound above is strict:

$$W_2^2(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z}, \Gamma) < \sum_{i=1}^d b_i^2\, W_2^2(Z_i, \Gamma)$$

  • Proof idea: couple the mixture and a Gaussian target through a shared noise vector $\mathbf{N}$; cross terms vanish by independence. The strict version builds on this bound, asking when equality could hold (full proofs: appendix).
  • Together: the global maximum of $W_2^2$ over $\mathcal{O}$ is attained only at a pure source direction.

Seeing it happen: mixing two sources

Laplace density (unit variance): $f_L(x) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}e^{-\sqrt2|x|}$

Uniform density (unit variance): $f_U(x) = \tfrac{1}{2\sqrt3}, \ x\in[-\sqrt3,\sqrt3]$

General mixture: $\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z} = \sum_i b_i Z_i$

Here, two sources on the unit circle: $y(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = s_1\cos\boldsymbol{\theta} + s_2\sin\boldsymbol{\theta},\ \ s_1\!\sim\! f_L,\ s_2\!\sim\! f_U$

  • CLT: the mixture $y(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ looks more Gaussian than either pure source.
  • $W_2^2$ provides the contrast: it dips toward the mixture and peaks at the pure sources.

Every proxy above replaces the true non-Gaussianity $G(Y_i)$ with something estimable from low-order statistics or a fixed density assumption; each fails on specific source geometries (next: what that looks like).

Optimization with $W_2^2$

  • Maximizing $W_2^2$ over $\mathbf{b}\in\mathcal{O}$ finds only the single most non-Gaussian direction; recovering all $d$ sources needs an iterative mechanism.
  • Iterative deflation: after finding $\mathbf{b}_1$, restrict the next search to directions orthogonal to it ($\mathbf{b}_2^\top\mathbf{b}_1=0$); for the $k$-th component, restrict to orthogonal to all $\mathbf{b}_j,\, j \lt k$.
  • Why this is valid, not just convenient: because $\mathbf{B}$ is constrained to the orthogonal group, the true recovery directions are already mutually orthogonal. Restricting subsequent search to the orthogonal complement of what's already found cannot exclude the true remaining sources.
  • The catch: naive sequential deflation lets any error in $\mathbf{b}_1$ propagate into the orthogonal complement used to search for $\mathbf{b}_2$, and so on, errors accumulate down the chain.
  • Fix, joint symmetric optimization: treat the full matrix $\mathbf{B}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ as one point on $\mathcal{O}$ and update all $d$ rows together. Every step retracts the whole matrix at once via symmetric decorrelation, $\mathbf{B}_{\text{new}} = (\mathbf{B}\mathbf{B}^\top)^{-1/2}\mathbf{B}$, which spreads any orthogonality violation evenly across all components rather than fixing earlier ones and adjusting the rest around them (as e.g. Gram-Schmidt would).

Making $W_2^2$ optimization practical

In the order they're applied, once per optimization step (analytical targets are precomputed once):

TechniqueWhat it solves
Analytical Gaussian targetsprecomputed once: replaces sampled quantile targets with their exact bin expectation, removing target-approximation noise
Batched restarts + stochastic mini-batchingparallelizes random restarts and subsamples each step, helping escape spurious local optima in non-convex/discrete landscapes
Gaussian ditheringinjects small noise ($\sigma^2{=}0.01$) into the batch before sorting, smoothing discrete step-function CDFs into differentiable curves [Schuchman 1964]
Riemannian gradient ascentprojects the Euclidean gradient onto the tangent space of $\mathcal{O}$, then steps along it, respecting the orthogonality constraint [Absil et al. 2008]
L-BFGS (quasi-Newton)refines that step: the exact Newton Hessian needs a density estimate at the quantile boundary, so L-BFGS approximates curvature from gradient history instead [Liu & Nocedal 1989]
Retraction (symmetric decorrelation)maps the stepped matrix back onto $\mathcal{O}$ via $\mathbf{C}^{-1/2}\mathbf{B}_{\text{step}}$, jointly and symmetrically across all components [Absil et al. 2008]

Application: EEG ocular-artifact removal [Hyvärinen et al. 2001]

  • The skull is a linear volume conductor: blink artifacts mix with neural signal at the scalp.
  • 5 frontal channels, MNE sample dataset; OT-ICA isolates the blink into one component, via excess kurtosis $\kappa\!\approx\!60$ vs. $\approx\!0$ for the rest, no density model assumed.
  • What RMS means: $\mathrm{RMS}=\sqrt{\tfrac1{|W|}\sum_{t\in W}x_t^2}$, the signal's power in a time window $W$. A blink injects a large transient that inflates RMS locally.
  • Result: zeroing that one component and reconstructing gives >90% RMS reduction in the blink window, leaving the rest of the recording untouched.
  • Why it matters: downstream EEG analysis (ERP studies, BCI, clinical reading) needs artifact-free signal; this removes the artifact without discarding the trial or fitting a density model.

Limitations and what's next

  • The case for OT-ICA: $W_2^2$ is a true metric on distributions, not a moment/likelihood proxy; this is why it generalizes better across heterogeneous mixtures where proxy methods are misspecified for at least one source type.
  • Limitation 1, discrete sources: step-function CDFs create gradient plateaus that stall the solver, even though the $W_2^2$ contrast signal is present. Gaussian dithering [Schuchman 1964] partially smooths this; Sinkhorn distances [Cuturi 2013] (entropic OT regularization) may smooth the transport plan further.
  • Limitation 2, speed: cost scales as $K\cdot dN\log N$ per iteration vs. FastICA's $dN$. Amortized Sinkhorn may help: instead of solving the entropic OT problem from scratch at every step, warm-start the dual potentials from the previous iteration's nearby rotation; consecutive steps query very similar projections, so the iterative cost is spread out ("amortized") across the optimization trajectory instead of paid in full every time.
  • Future work: OT-ICA as a front-end for LiNGAM [Shimizu et al. 2006], Causal Component Analysis [Liang et al. 2023], and identifiable nonlinear ICA [Buchholz et al. 2022].

Appendix A1

Proof: $W_2$ upper bound

Let $\mathbf{N}=(N_1,\dots,N_d)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I_d)$, independent of $\mathbf{Z}$. Let $T_i$ be the OT map with $(T_i)_\#\Gamma = Z_i$. Construct, from the same $\mathbf{N}$:

$$X = \sum_i b_i T_i(N_i), \qquad Y = \sum_i b_i N_i \sim \Gamma \ \ (\text{since } \textstyle\sum b_i^2=1).$$

$(X,Y)$ is a valid coupling of $\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z}$ and $\Gamma$, so $W_2^2 \le \mathbb{E}[|X-Y|^2]$. Expanding:

$$|X-Y|^2 = \sum_i b_i^2\big(T_i(N_i)-N_i\big)^2 + \sum_{i\ne j} b_i b_j\big(T_i(N_i)-N_i\big)\big(T_j(N_j)-N_j\big).$$

Cross terms vanish in expectation ($N_i \perp N_j$, zero mean). The diagonal terms are exactly $W_2^2(Z_i,\Gamma)$:

$$W_2^2(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z}, \Gamma) \le \sum_i b_i^2\, W_2^2(Z_i,\Gamma). \qquad \blacksquare$$
Appendix A2

Proof: strict inequality (global max = single source)

Appendix A1 established the upper bound $W_2^2(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z},\Gamma) \le \sum_i b_i^2\,W_2^2(Z_i,\Gamma)$ via the coupling $X=\sum_i b_i T_i(N_i)$, $Y=\sum_i b_i N_i$. We now use that same coupling as the starting point and ask: when is it tight?

Equality in 1D $W_2^2$ holds iff the two variables are comonotonic (Brenier): $\nabla X(\mathbf{N}) = \lambda(\mathbf{N})\,\nabla Y(\mathbf{N})$.

By Caffarelli regularity, $T_i$ are $C^1$. Differentiating again: the Hessian of $X$ is diagonal ($b_i T_i''(N_i)$ on the diagonal, since $X$ separates additively), while the Hessian of $\lambda(\mathbf{N})\mathbf{b}$ is the rank-1 outer product $\mathbf{b}(\nabla\lambda)^\top$.

Off-diagonal entries: LHS is $0$, RHS is $b_i\,\partial\lambda/\partial N_j$. Since multiple $b_i\ne 0$ in a true mixture, $\nabla\lambda \equiv 0$, so $\lambda$ is a constant and $T_i(x)=\lambda x + c$, a linear map.

A linear map of Gaussian noise is Gaussian. But ICA identifiability assumes $Z_i$ non-Gaussian, a contradiction. So equality cannot hold for a true mixture of non-Gaussian sources, and the bound from A1 is strict:

$$W_2^2(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z},\Gamma) < \sum_i b_i^2\,W_2^2(Z_i,\Gamma) \quad\text{whenever} \ge 2 \text{ weights } b_i\ne 0. \qquad \blacksquare$$

Consequence: the maximum of $W_2^2(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{Z},\Gamma)$ over $\mathbf{b}\in\mathcal{O}$ is attained only when $\mathbf{b}$ aligns with a single source axis.

Appendix A3

The Amari Performance Index

Let $\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{B}_{\text{est}}\mathbf{A}$ be the global transfer matrix (estimated unmixing composed with the true mixing). Because ICA is identifiable only up to permutation and scaling, the target is a generalized permutation matrix, not the identity.

$$E(\mathbf{P}) = \frac{1}{2d}\sum_{i=1}^d\left(\sum_{j=1}^d \frac{|p_{ij}|}{\max_k|p_{ik}|} - 1\right) + \frac{1}{2d}\sum_{j=1}^d\left(\sum_{i=1}^d \frac{|p_{ij}|}{\max_k|p_{kj}|} - 1\right)$$

Amari error $E$Interpretation
$E<0.1$Excellent to near-perfect separation
$0.1\le E<0.3$Good separation; source structure highly recoverable
$0.3\le E<0.5$Moderate separation; some structural detail lost
